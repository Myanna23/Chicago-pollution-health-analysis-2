# Environmental Inequality in Chicago: Pollution, Complaints & Health Outcomes

## The Question
Do low-income Chicago communities face disproportionate exposure 
to environmental hazards, and how does that connect to chronic 
illness rates in those same neighborhoods?

## Why It Matters
Working as an Administrative Assistant at Equus Workforce Solutions 
in Chicago, I saw firsthand how environmental and economic hardship 
overlap in certain communities. I wanted to understand the data 
behind those patterns.

## Data Sources
- EPA ECHO Database — industrial facility locations and violations
- CDPH Environmental Complaints (City of Chicago) — 64,675 resident 
  complaints by type and location
- CDPH Environmental Enforcement — 37,824 violation and fine records
- CDC PLACES 2025 — chronic disease prevalence by Cook County 
  census tract

## What I Found
- Air pollution work orders, noise complaints, and illegal dumping 
  were the top 3 environmental complaints, consistent with 
  industrial facility proximity in residential neighborhoods
- High blood pressure, diabetes, and asthma were the most prevalent 
  health conditions across affected census tracts, all with 
  established links to pollution exposure
- Complaint density is visibly concentrated in Chicago's South and 
  West Side neighborhoods

## What I'd Do Differently
I'd incorporate CDC USALEEP life expectancy data by census tract 
and break the analysis down to census tract level instead of 
relying on citywide complaint coordinates. Zip code and tract level 
poverty data from the Census would also strengthen the connection 
between income, industrial density, and health outcomes.
